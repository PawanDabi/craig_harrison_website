On the Architecture of Silence

The most powerful spaces in architecture are often the quietest. Not the grand gesture, not the spectacle, but the room that slows your breath the moment you enter — the threshold that makes you pause before you cross. This is not accident. It is the result of profound restraint.

In this essay, Craig Harrison explores the tradition of spatial quietness in European architecture — from the austere interiors of Nordic churches to the meditative voids of Japanese teahouses — and its relevance to contemporary British practice.
